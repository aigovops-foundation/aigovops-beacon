# Engagement protocols (v0.1 — Steward to finalize)

This document is the question we are asking the Founding Steward Partner — Glacis — to answer:

> **How does a company engage with the AiGovOps Foundation in a way that is helpful to commercial stewards (you, future stewards, and other vendor-members) and helpful to the community at the same time?**

We have studied the engagement models used by the [FinOps Foundation](https://www.finops.org/about/governing-board/), [CNCF](https://github.com/cncf/foundation/blob/main/charter.md), and the [Linux Foundation](https://www.linuxfoundation.org/projects/hosting). All three converge on a small number of moves that, run together, generate a virtuous cycle between vendor investment and community trust. We do not assume any of them is correct for this space without the Steward's review.

This document is a **placeholder**. We are publishing it so Joe and Glacis can revise, replace, or supersede it.

## The question, broken into pieces

The Steward is asked to define, in writing:

1. **How do new commercial companies join the program?**
   - Membership tiers? Dues structure? Voting weight (we propose zero — see [`GOVERNANCE.md`](GOVERNANCE.md))?
   - What does a Founding Member get that a regular member does not?
   - How does membership intersect with profile registration?

2. **How do member companies contribute technically without dominating?**
   - Maintainer eligibility from member companies vs. independents.
   - Cooling-off period between commercial release and Foundation-affiliated implementation.
   - Conflict-of-interest disclosure cadence and form.

3. **How do member companies surface commercial offerings to the community?**
   - Allowed: case studies, conference talks, sponsored research, deployment partnerships.
   - Disallowed: gating community resources behind commercial paywalls, "preferred vendor" lists, exclusive integrations.
   - The line between "showcased" and "endorsed."

4. **How does the Foundation surface commercial offerings *for* member companies?**
   - Co-marketing rules (see FinOps's published examples).
   - Sponsored content disclosure.
   - Newsletter / event / podcast slot allocation.

5. **How are conflicts resolved when two member companies want to take the same project in different directions?**
   - TRC adjudication procedure.
   - Forking rules.
   - Brand-use rules in a fork.

6. **What does "graduating" a profile look like?**
   - From in-progress → AAL-1 self-declared → AAL-2 with IAP → AAL-3 / AAL-4.
   - Gate criteria the TRC applies at each step.
   - Co-marketing the graduation event.

7. **How do we engage *with the Steward's commercial business* fairly?**
   - This is the question we most need Joe to answer in his own words.
   - We want the Foundation to be a multiplier for Glacis's commercial work (and for every future steward / member's commercial work), not a competitor.
   - We also want the community to trust that the Foundation is not a marketing arm of any one company.
   - The answer to this is the difference between FinOps (works) and a captured industry consortium (doesn't).

## What we propose as starting points (for Joe to throw away or refine)

### Membership tiers (placeholder)
- **Founding Steward** (Glacis, 1 seat): defined in [`STEWARD.md`](STEWARD.md).
- **Steward Council** (3–7 seats): companies whose commercial product is a conformant OVERT implementation or directly complementary. Annual dues to be set by Governing Board. No technical-track vote.
- **Implementer Members** (open): companies running Beacon or a future Foundation project in production. Modest annual dues. Access to private slack, pre-release builds, case-study slots.
- **Practitioner Members** (free): individuals. Full community access. No vote, no dues.
- **Academic / Civil Society Members** (free): for researchers, journalists, civil-society auditors. Full access.

### The five things every member company gets (placeholder)
1. Logo placement on the Foundation site, tier-tagged.
2. Annual case-study slot (subject to editorial review by the TRC).
3. Co-marketing rights for any Foundation-affiliated project they ship.
4. Pre-release access to Foundation projects in their tier or above.
5. Speaker priority at one Foundation event per year.

### The five things no member company gets (placeholder, but firm)
1. Veto over Foundation project direction.
2. Exclusive integration rights with any Foundation project.
3. "Preferred vendor" or "Foundation-endorsed" status outside the Steward Partner role.
4. The ability to gate any community resource behind their paywall.
5. The ability to remove a contribution from a competitor on commercial grounds.

### The hard rule
> **No member company — including the Founding Steward — ships a Foundation-affiliated implementation publicly without TRC pre-release review.** This rule exists because we violated its spirit in May 2026, and the rule is the structural answer to "never again."
