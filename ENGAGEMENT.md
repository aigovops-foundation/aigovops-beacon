# Engagement — how companies and people get involved

This is the question we are asking the founding Project Steward — Glacis — to answer for us:

> **How does a company get involved with the AIGovOps Foundation in a way that is helpful to the steward, helpful to other companies in the space, and helpful to the community at the same time?**

Open-source projects with commercial gravity have figured this out before. The [FinOps Foundation](https://www.finops.org/), [CNCF](https://www.cncf.io/), and [Linux Foundation](https://www.linuxfoundation.org/projects/hosting) playbooks all converge on a few moves that, run together, create a virtuous loop between vendor investment and community trust. We have read those playbooks; we have not earned the right to recite them.

So this doc is two things: a set of questions we are inviting Joe and Glacis to answer in their voice, and a starting sketch they are welcome to throw away.

---

## Questions we want the steward to answer

These are the ones that matter most. We'd love Joe's voice on them, on the record, replacing the sketches below:

1. **How does a new company get involved?** Tiers, dues, what you get for joining, how membership intersects with profile registration — what's the shape the steward thinks works in the AI governance space?
2. **How do member companies contribute without dominating?** Where's the line between "earning maintainer trust" and "leveraging a commercial team into the project"? When is a cooling-off period right? When is it overkill?
3. **How do member companies talk about their commercial products in community channels?** Case studies feel fine. "Preferred vendor" feels not fine. Where's the line and how do we keep it without policing tone?
4. **How does the Foundation surface commercial offerings *for* members?** Joint launches, conference slots, podcast slots, co-marketing rules. What feels generous without feeling captured?
5. **What does the steward need so the Foundation does not undercut Glacis's commercial roadmap?** And what does the community need so the steward's commercial position does not get used to suppress contributions either?
6. **What "no surprises" norm should bind every member company — including future stewards?** A heads-up before an OVERT-adjacent commercial release? How many days? With what artifacts? Is it a habit or a rule, and how do we keep it without turning it into paperwork?
7. **What does the review circle look at, in checklist form, before a Foundation project ships?**
8. **When is it the right moment to invite a second steward?** Not now. But how do we know when?

We are not asking you to answer all eight on the call. We are asking you to take a first pass at the ones that feel ripe, in writing, when it suits you. We will publish what you write.

## A starting sketch — for the steward to keep, change, or throw out

### Three doors in

**Use it.** Try Beacon. Stand it up. File the issues you hit. That is contribution.

**Build it.** Send a PR. Earn maintainer trust. Help review the work. The maintainer ladder is in [`CONTRIBUTING.md`](CONTRIBUTING.md).

**Back it.** Companies whose work touches the standard can sponsor the Foundation. Sponsorship pays for events, infrastructure, and the community team. It does not buy a seat on the review circle and it does not buy a faster merge.

### How a company "joins"

For now, "joining" means: your name on the sponsor page, an annual case-study slot, co-marketing rights for any Foundation project you ship in production, pre-release access to projects you sponsor, and one speaker slot per year at a Foundation event. Modest. Useful. Not extravagant.

If the steward thinks tiered membership makes sense — Implementer, Steward Council, Practitioner, Academic — we'll build it that way. That call is yours.

### What sponsorship does not buy

- A say in what the project does.
- Exclusive integrations.
- "Foundation-endorsed" status for a commercial product.
- The ability to gate any community resource behind a paywall.
- The ability to ask a maintainer to remove a competitor's contribution.

These are habits we plan to keep whether or not anyone signs anything. If we ever have to say "no" to a sponsor on one of these, we'd rather have written it down first.

### The one habit we are firm about

> **No Foundation-affiliated implementation ships publicly without the steward and the review circle seeing it first.**

We did the opposite of this in April. We learned. The habit is the lesson, kept in the open.

## Contributor norms

A few small things that make the project more pleasant to work in. They are not novel; they are borrowed from communities we admire.

- **Issues are the front door.** If you're unsure whether to send a PR, open an issue first.
- **Small PRs beat big PRs.** Easier to review, easier to revert, easier to celebrate.
- **Helpful commit messages.** Future-you will be grateful.
- **Kindness is non-negotiable.** See [`CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md`](CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md).
- **Reviews are gifts.** If someone reviews your PR, thank them. If you review someone's PR, be the reviewer you would want.
- **Disagree in the open.** Side channels are for sympathy, not strategy.
- **Step back when you need to.** Burnout is the most common way open-source projects lose people. We'd rather lose a release than a contributor.
